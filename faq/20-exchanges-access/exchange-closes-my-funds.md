**Short answer.** Act on the exchange's official instructions promptly: closures normally announce a withdrawal window (Orange Gateway's 2026 notice gave 60 days, [[orange-gateway-mica]]). Withdraw coins to a wallet whose keys you control, verify the address and network carefully, and be intensely suspicious of anyone contacting *you* about the closure — closures attract scammers.

## More detail

A practical sequence:

1. **Confirm the news at the source** — the exchange's own site or verified account, not screenshots or messages sent to you. Scammers exploit closure panic with fake "expedited withdrawal" sites ([[keeping-bsv-safe]]).
2. **Set up self-custody first** if you don't have it: install a reputable wallet, back up the recovery phrase offline ([[what-is-a-wallet]], [[custodial-vs-own-wallet]]).
3. **Withdraw within the window.** Verify the address and the network before confirming ([[sending-bsv-basics]]); do a small test withdrawal first. Missing the window usually means your claim moves into a slower legal wind-down process.
4. **Export your records** — transaction history and statements — while the account is accessible; you may need them for taxes or claims.

For context on *why* closures happen: often it's licensing — an exchange that can't obtain a required authorization must stop serving that market ([[why-do-licenses-matter]], [[what-is-mica]]). An announced wind-down with a withdrawal window is an orderly process with a defined path for customers; a sudden insolvency is not — which is the practical difference between the two for anyone holding funds there.

Worth restating: your coins on an exchange are that company's custody, which is exactly why this page exists. Coins already in your own wallet are untouched by any exchange's fate ([[exchange-problems-mean-bsv-problems]]).
