**Short answer.** An exchange is a private company that runs a marketplace where people trade digital assets against regular money or each other. It's the usual bridge between the banking world and blockchains — and it is a business with its own owners, rules, licenses, and risks, entirely separate from the blockchains whose coins it trades.

## More detail

Functionally, an exchange does three things: holds customer deposits (money and coins — making it a custodian, [[custodial-vs-own-wallet]]), matches buyers with sellers, and connects to banking rails so you can move regular money in and out ([[what-are-on-off-ramps]]).

The separateness is the point beginners most often miss. An exchange listing BSV is a commercial decision by that company ([[what-is-a-listing]]); an exchange closing is a company event ([[exchange-closes-my-funds]], [[orange-gateway-mica]]). None of it changes the blockchain itself, which runs independently of any venue trading its coin ([[exchange-problems-mean-bsv-problems]]).

Because exchanges touch customer money, they're regulated financial businesses in most jurisdictions ([[who-regulates-crypto]]). Licenses determine where they may operate and which customers they may serve — which is why BSV's availability differs by country and changes over time ([[where-to-get-bsv]], [[why-do-licenses-matter]]).
