**Short answer.** To receive: open your wallet and share the address or QR code it shows you. To send: paste or scan the other person's address, enter the amount, and confirm. The payment reaches them in seconds and costs a fraction of a cent. One rule matters most: check the address and the network before you confirm, because blockchain payments cannot be undone.

## More detail

Addresses are long on purpose, so that typing mistakes get caught. But an address that is wrong yet still valid will not be caught — for example, from a bad paste, or from harmful software that swaps what you copied. Good habits: scan QR codes instead of typing, check the first and last few characters after pasting, and send a small test amount first when the sum is significant.

**Payments cannot be reversed.** That is how the system is meant to work, not a missing safety net. No company can pull back a confirmed payment. It is what makes the network trustworthy for the person receiving money — and unforgiving of a sender's mistakes.

**Check the network.** BSV addresses look similar to those of related blockchains ([[bitcoin-vs-bsv]]). Sending BSV to an address on a different network typically loses the coins. Wallets and exchanges label the network — read the label.

Some BSV wallets also let you pay a simple name instead of a long address, using standards such as Paymail. The same rule applies: check before you confirm.
