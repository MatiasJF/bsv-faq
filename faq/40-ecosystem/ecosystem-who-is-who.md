**Short answer.** Three separate groups, easy to mix up: the **BSV Association** (a Swiss non-profit that looks after the technology's rules), the **independent businesses that process payments** on the network, and **independent companies** — exchanges, wallet makers, app builders, and news sites, each owned and run separately. When something happens in the news, knowing which group did it answers most "why did this happen?" questions.

## More detail

The map, and what each group actually decides:

| Who | What they are | What they decide |
|---|---|---|
| BSV Association | A Swiss non-profit ([[what-is-the-bsv-association]]) | The software and rules of the system, standards, education — not how the network is run day to day ([[what-bsva-does-not-do]]) |
| Payment processors | Independent businesses ([[who-runs-the-bsv-network]]) | Whether to take part, and what equipment to run |
| Independent companies | Exchanges, wallets, apps, media | Their own products, what they list ([[what-is-a-listing]]), their own licenses ([[why-do-licenses-matter]]), what they publish |

The last row matters most. The exchanges that trade BSV, the wallets that hold it, and the websites that write about it — even the very enthusiastic ones — are all independent firms. What they say is their own opinion, and what happens to their business is their own story ([[orange-gateway-mica]]). None of them speaks for the technology or for the Association ([[exchange-problems-mean-bsv-problems]]).

A useful habit when you read BSV news: first ask, "which of these groups is this about?" A story about one company — a closure, a dispute, a bold claim — is very different from a story about the system everyone shares ([[is-bsv-a-company]]).
