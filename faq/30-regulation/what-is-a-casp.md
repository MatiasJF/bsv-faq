**Short answer.** CASP stands for "crypto-asset service provider". It is the EU's legal name, under the MiCA law, for a company that offers crypto services to customers. That covers running an exchange, keeping customers' coins for them, carrying out buy and sell orders, giving advice, or moving assets for clients. Think of it like a bureau de change: a business that handles other people's money needs a permit. If a company does these things for customers in the EU or the wider EEA (the EU plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Norway), it needs official permission — a CASP authorization.

## More detail

The label is about what a company *does*, not the technology it uses. A company becomes a CASP through the business it runs — keeping customers' coins for them ([[custodial-vs-own-wallet]]), running a trading venue ([[what-is-an-exchange]]), or swapping crypto for regular money ([[what-are-on-off-ramps]]) — no matter which blockchains it supports.

Getting that permission works broadly like this: the financial watchdog in the company's country checks its financial reserves, how it is run, how it keeps customers' coins safe, and how it handles complaints. That watchdog is BaFin in Germany, the AMF in France, and — for Iceland, an EEA country that applies the EU's rules — the Central Bank of Iceland. Once granted, the license is valid across the whole EU/EEA. The watchdog can also say no, or later withdraw a license it has given — the situation Orange Gateway reported in 2026 ([[orange-gateway-mica]]).

What is *not* a CASP matters too. Making wallet software without holding users' keys, running the blockchain's machinery ([[who-runs-the-bsv-network]]), or publishing about crypto are generally not activities that need this license. That is why the same event can close an exchange while the network — and people holding their own coins — carry on untouched ([[does-regulation-affect-my-coins]]).
