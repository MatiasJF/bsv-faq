**Short answer.** CASP stands for "crypto-asset service provider" — the EU's legal term, under MiCA, for a company that provides crypto services to customers: running an exchange, holding coins in custody, executing orders, advising, or transferring assets on clients' behalf. If a business does these things for customers in the EU or the wider EEA (the EU plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Norway), it needs a CASP authorization.

## More detail

The label is about *activities*, not about technology. A company becomes a CASP by what it does commercially — taking custody of customers' coins ([[custodial-vs-own-wallet]]), operating a trading venue ([[what-is-an-exchange]]), exchanging crypto for regular money ([[what-are-on-off-ramps]]) — regardless of which blockchains it supports.

What authorization involves, in broad strokes: a national regulator reviews the company's capital, governance, custody arrangements, and complaint handling — BaFin in Germany, the AMF in France, and for Iceland (an EEA country applying the EU's rules) the Central Bank of Iceland. Once granted, the license is valid across the whole EU/EEA. The regulator can also refuse or revoke it — the situation Orange Gateway reported in 2026 ([[orange-gateway-mica]]).

What's *not* a CASP matters too: writing wallet software without holding user keys, running blockchain infrastructure ([[who-runs-the-bsv-network]]), or publishing about crypto are generally not licensable CASP activities. This is why the same event can close an exchange while leaving the network and self-custody users untouched ([[does-regulation-affect-my-coins]]).
