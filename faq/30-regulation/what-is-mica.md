**Short answer.** MiCA (short for "Markets in Crypto-Assets") is the European Union's law for crypto businesses. Since it took full effect, companies that hold, trade, or manage customers' digital assets must have a license — putting them under rules similar in spirit to those for other financial services. The transition period ended on 1 July 2026. After that date, providers without a license had to stop serving customers in the EU/EEA.

## More detail

MiCA covers the *companies that provide services* — exchanges, businesses that hold coins for customers, and brokers; the law calls them CASPs ([[what-is-a-casp]]). It does not cover the blockchains themselves ([[does-regulation-affect-my-coins]]). Its stated aims are the familiar ones of financial rules: protecting customers' money, keeping trading fair, and making clear who is responsible. The rules are the same in every EU and EEA country — one rulebook for the whole bloc instead of a different one in each country. That is why an Icelandic exchange answers to it through Iceland's central bank ([[orange-gateway-mica]]).

What matters day to day for you: a company needs a license from the financial authority in one member state, and that license then counts across the whole bloc. Existing businesses were given a transition period to get licensed. The final deadline of that period was 1 July 2026, and industry reporting around the deadline indicated that most European crypto firms had not yet obtained a license. That made mid-2026 a period of visible reshaping of the market, with some platforms winding down and users moving to licensed ones.

For BSV specifically, MiCA is neither for nor against any particular blockchain. It decides *which companies* may offer services in Europe — and so it shapes where Europeans can buy, sell, or keep their BSV with a company ([[where-to-get-bsv]], [[what-are-on-off-ramps]]).
