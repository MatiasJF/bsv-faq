**Short answer.** MiCA ("Markets in Crypto-Assets") is the European Union's regulation for crypto businesses. Since taking full effect, it requires companies that hold, trade, or manage customers' digital assets to be licensed — bringing them under rules similar in spirit to other financial services. After the transition period ended on 1 July 2026, unlicensed providers had to stop serving EU/EEA customers.

## More detail

MiCA regulates *service providers* — exchanges, custodians, brokers, called CASPs ([[what-is-a-casp]]) — not the blockchains themselves ([[does-regulation-affect-my-coins]]). Its stated aims are the usual ones of financial regulation: protecting customer funds, market integrity, and clear accountability, applied uniformly across all EU and EEA countries (which is why an Icelandic exchange answers to it through Iceland's central bank, [[orange-gateway-mica]]).

The mechanics that matter to an ordinary user: a company needs authorization from a national regulator in one member state, and that license then "passports" across the whole bloc. Existing businesses were given a transition period to obtain licenses; that period's final deadline was 1 July 2026, and industry reporting around the deadline indicated most European crypto firms had not yet obtained one — making mid-2026 a period of visible consolidation, with some platforms winding down and users migrating to licensed venues.

For BSV specifically, MiCA is neither pro- nor anti- any particular blockchain: it decides *which companies* may offer services in Europe, and therefore shapes where Europeans can buy, sell, or custody BSV ([[where-to-get-bsv]], [[what-are-on-off-ramps]]).
