**Short answer.** Because companies that hold other people's money are exactly where things historically go wrong. A license means a regulator has examined the company's finances, governance, and custody arrangements and holds it accountable on an ongoing basis. For users, "licensed for my country" is the closest thing to a baseline safety check when choosing where to trade or custody assets.

## More detail

What a license actually buys a customer, in concrete terms: capital requirements (the company must hold reserves), segregation rules (customer assets kept apart from company money), audit and reporting duties, complaint procedures, and a regulator with the power to intervene. None of it makes a company failure impossible — it makes failures rarer and more orderly, with wind-down processes instead of vanishing acts ([[exchange-closes-my-funds]]).

The flip side: a company that can't obtain a license must stop offering the regulated services in that market — however good or bad its product otherwise is. That's the mechanism behind mid-2026's wave of European platform closures after the MiCA deadline ([[what-is-mica]], [[orange-gateway-mica]]): a legal cutoff, applied industry-wide, regardless of which coins a venue traded.

Two boundaries keep expectations honest. Licenses attach to *companies*, so a licensing failure is a company event, not a blockchain event ([[does-regulation-affect-my-coins]]). And regulation covers *custodial services* — coins in your own wallet involve no license because no company holds anything on your behalf ([[custodial-vs-own-wallet]]).
