**Short answer.** It depends on where the *company* serving you operates — there is no single worldwide crypto watchdog. In the EU/EEA, each country's financial authority applies the shared EU rulebook, called MiCA. In Switzerland it is FINMA. In the UK it is the FCA. In the US, several agencies share the job. The blockchain networks themselves are not licensed by anyone — the rules apply to businesses and what they do.

## More detail

The pattern is much the same everywhere, even where the details differ. Businesses that hold or move *other people's assets* — exchanges, companies that keep coins for customers, brokers, and services that swap coins for bank money ([[what-are-on-off-ramps]]) — need permission from the local financial watchdog. Nearly everywhere, they must also follow rules against money laundering.

A quick map (as of mid-2026):

- **EU/EEA**: one rulebook, MiCA ([[what-is-mica]]), applied by each country's own authority — BaFin (Germany), the AMF (France), the Central Bank of Iceland, and so on — with licenses valid across the whole bloc ([[what-is-a-casp]]).
- **Switzerland**: FINMA watches over financial activities; crypto services fall under the existing financial-market laws rather than a separate crypto law.
- **UK, Singapore, Japan, UAE and others**: their own sets of crypto rules, of varying scope, run by their financial authorities.
- **US**: several federal and state bodies (SEC, CFTC, FinCEN, state regulators), with the lines between them still shifting.

Because a license is granted country by country, the same service can be available in one country and not in the one next door. That is the main reason "where can I get BSV" has no single global answer ([[where-to-get-bsv]]) — and why deadlines in the rules change which platforms are open to you ([[orange-gateway-mica]]).
