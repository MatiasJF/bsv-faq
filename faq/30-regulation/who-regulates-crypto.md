**Short answer.** It depends where the *company* serving you operates — there is no global crypto regulator. In the EU/EEA it's national financial authorities applying the shared MiCA rulebook; in Switzerland it's FINMA; in the UK the FCA; in the US several agencies share the ground. The blockchain networks themselves aren't licensed by anyone — regulation attaches to businesses and their activities.

## More detail

The pattern across jurisdictions is consistent even where the details differ: activities that involve holding or moving *other people's assets* — exchanges, custodians, brokers, payment ramps ([[what-are-on-off-ramps]]) — require authorization from the local financial supervisor, plus anti-money-laundering compliance nearly everywhere.

A quick orientation map (as of mid-2026):

- **EU/EEA**: one rulebook, MiCA ([[what-is-mica]]), enforced by each country's authority — BaFin (Germany), AMF (France), Central Bank of Iceland, and so on — with licenses valid across the whole bloc ([[what-is-a-casp]]).
- **Switzerland**: FINMA supervises financial activities; crypto services fall under existing financial-market laws rather than a dedicated crypto act.
- **UK, Singapore, Japan, UAE and others**: dedicated regimes of varying scope run by their financial authorities.
- **US**: multiple federal and state bodies (SEC, CFTC, FinCEN, state regulators) with boundaries that continue to evolve.

Because a license is per-jurisdiction, the same service can be available in one country and not its neighbor — the main reason "where can I get BSV" has no single global answer ([[where-to-get-bsv]]) and why regulatory deadlines reshape the venue map ([[orange-gateway-mica]]).
