**Short answer.** BSV emerged in November 2018 from a split ("fork") of Bitcoin Cash, which had itself split from Bitcoin in 2017. Each split happened because participants disagreed about the rules the network should follow — mainly how large blocks should be and how closely to keep to the original Bitcoin protocol. BSV's stated goal was to restore the original protocol design and scale it without limits.

## More detail

Open blockchains have no owner, so when their communities disagree fundamentally, the software can split: both groups keep a copy of the shared history up to that moment, then continue under different rules as separate networks. Holders of the original coin end up with coins on both resulting chains.

That happened twice in BSV's lineage. In August 2017, Bitcoin split into BTC and Bitcoin Cash (BCH) over how to increase transaction capacity. In November 2018, Bitcoin Cash split again into BCH and BSV over how far and how fast to scale, and over whether to keep modifying the protocol or lock it close to the original design.

The chains have developed separately since. BTC, BCH, and BSV share early history and similar technology but are different networks with different coins, different rules, and different communities ([[bitcoin-vs-bsv]]). "BSV" is commonly read as "Bitcoin Satoshi Vision," reflecting the aim its supporters state: implementing the design described in the original Bitcoin white paper, with unbounded scaling ([[what-is-scaling]]).

This FAQ describes each network's stated goals without ranking them — which chain "is the real Bitcoin" is a position people hold, not a fact a neutral source can declare ([[bitcoin-vs-bsv]]).
