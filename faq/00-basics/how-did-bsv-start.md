**Short answer.** BSV appeared in November 2018, when a network called Bitcoin Cash split in two — a split known as a "fork." Bitcoin Cash had itself split off from Bitcoin in 2017. Each split happened because people in the community disagreed about the rules the network should follow — mainly how large the batches of payments (blocks) should be, and how closely to stick to Bitcoin's original design. BSV's stated goal was to bring back that original design and grow it without limits.

## More detail

An open blockchain has no owner. So when its community disagrees deeply, the system can split. Both groups keep a copy of the shared history up to that moment. Then each carries on under its own rules, as a separate network. Anyone who held the original coin ends up with coins on both resulting chains.

That happened twice in BSV's history. In August 2017, Bitcoin split into BTC and Bitcoin Cash (BCH). That disagreement was about how to handle more payments. In November 2018, Bitcoin Cash split again, into BCH and BSV. That disagreement was about how far and how fast to grow, and whether to keep changing the rules or lock them close to the original design.

The chains have gone their own ways since. BTC, BCH, and BSV share their early history and similar technology, but they are different networks with different coins, different rules, and different communities ([[bitcoin-vs-bsv]]). "BSV" is commonly read as "Bitcoin Satoshi Vision." That reflects the aim its supporters state: to build the design described in the original Bitcoin paper, with no limit on growth ([[what-is-scaling]]).

This FAQ describes what each network says it aims for, without ranking them. Which chain "is the real Bitcoin" is a position people hold, not a fact a neutral source can declare ([[bitcoin-vs-bsv]]).
