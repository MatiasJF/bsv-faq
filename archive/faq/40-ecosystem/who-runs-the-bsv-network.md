**Short answer.** Independent businesses, in various countries, run the BSV network. They operate the computers that check and record everyone's payments, and they earn fees and newly issued coins for that work. People often call them miners ([[what-is-mining]]). Anyone may join — no permission needed — and no single organization, including the BSV Association, runs the network itself.

## More detail

What these businesses do: they gather the payments people send, check that each one follows the rules, record them in the shared public record book ([[what-is-a-blockchain]]), and publish the result for everyone to see. They are paid for this work ([[what-are-fees]]).

Why you can trust the result: many of these businesses compete, and each keeps its own copy of the record book. If one tried to cheat its copy, everyone else's copies would disagree — so cheating gets a business nothing.

The wider picture divides neatly ([[ecosystem-who-is-who]]): these processors run the network; the BSV Association maintains the software most of them choose to use ([[what-is-the-bsv-association]], [[how-is-bsv-developed]]); and companies like exchanges and wallet apps build services on top ([[what-is-an-exchange]]). Each layer makes its own decisions — so "who decided this?" always has a specific answer, and it is rarely "the network" ([[what-bsva-does-not-do]]).

You don't have to take anyone's word that the network is running: its activity is public, and anyone can look at it live on viewing sites such as WhatsOnChain ([[is-bsv-dead]]).
