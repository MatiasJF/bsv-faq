**Short answer.** An exchange is a private company that runs a marketplace. People go there to swap digital coins for regular money, or for other coins. Think of a currency-exchange booth that also offers lockers. It is the usual doorway between the banking world and blockchains. But it is a business — with its own owners, rules, licenses, and risks. It is entirely separate from the blockchains whose coins it trades.

## More detail

An exchange does three things. It holds what customers deposit — money and coins — so the exchange holds your coins for you ([[custodial-vs-own-wallet]]). It matches people who want to buy with people who want to sell. And it connects to banks, so you can move regular money in and out ([[what-are-on-off-ramps]]).

That separateness is what newcomers most often miss. When an exchange offers BSV, that is a business choice made by that company ([[what-is-a-listing]]). When an exchange closes, that is a company event ([[exchange-closes-my-funds]], [[orange-gateway-mica]]). None of this changes the blockchain itself. It keeps running no matter which marketplace trades its coin ([[exchange-problems-mean-bsv-problems]]).

Because exchanges handle customer money, most countries treat them as regulated financial businesses ([[who-regulates-crypto]]). Licenses decide where an exchange may operate and which customers it may serve. That is why the places where you can get BSV differ by country and change over time ([[where-to-get-bsv]], [[why-do-licenses-matter]]).
