**Short answer.** For their own business or legal reasons. Maybe too few customers trade the coin to make it worth the upkeep. Maybe the rules in some country changed. Maybe the exchange simply changed direction. A delisting is the exchange deciding what to keep in its own shop — it does not switch the coin off, and you can still move your coins to your own wallet.

## More detail

The common reasons, across the whole industry:

- **Money.** Every coin on the shelf costs the exchange staff time and upkeep. If very few people trade it, it may not pay its way.
- **Rules.** An exchange's license limits what it may offer in each country ([[why-do-licenses-matter]]). When new rules arrive — like Europe's MiCA ([[what-is-mica]]) — exchanges re-check their shelves, and one without the required license has to stop serving those customers entirely ([[orange-gateway-mica]]).
- **Caution.** Exchanges regularly review the coins they carry and drop ones they are no longer comfortable with, by their own standards.
- **Business changes.** Partnerships and priorities shift, like in any industry.

For you as a holder, a delisting works like this: trading of that coin stops on that exchange, and the exchange announces a period during which you can withdraw your coins to your own wallet ([[exchange-closes-my-funds]]). The coin itself keeps working, and coins you hold in your own wallet are not affected ([[exchange-problems-mean-bsv-problems]]).

BSV has been both delisted and newly listed by exchanges over the years. In each case, the exchange explained its own reasons — and those statements are the best source on why ([[ecosystem-who-is-who]]). The one constant: listing decisions belong to the exchanges themselves, not to the organizations around BSV ([[what-bsva-does-not-do]]).
