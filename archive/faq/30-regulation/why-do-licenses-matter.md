**Short answer.** Because companies that hold other people's money are exactly where things have historically gone wrong. A license means a financial watchdog has examined the company's finances, how it is run, and how it keeps customers' assets — and keeps holding it to account afterwards. For you, "licensed for my country" is the closest thing to a basic safety check when choosing where to trade or where to leave your coins.

## More detail

What a license actually gives you as a customer, in plain terms: the company must hold money in reserve; it must keep customers' assets separate from its own money; it must be audited and report on itself; it must have a proper complaints procedure; and the watchdog has the power to step in. None of this makes a company failure impossible. It makes failures rarer and more orderly — an organised wind-down instead of a vanishing act ([[exchange-closes-my-funds]]).

The flip side: a company that cannot obtain a license must stop offering the regulated services in that market — however good or bad its product otherwise is. That is what drove the wave of European platform closures in mid-2026, after the MiCA deadline ([[what-is-mica]], [[orange-gateway-mica]]): one legal cut-off date, applied across the whole industry, no matter which coins a platform traded.

Two things keep expectations honest. Licenses belong to *companies*, so a license problem is a company event, not a blockchain event ([[does-regulation-affect-my-coins]]). And the rules cover services that hold things *for* you — coins in your own wallet involve no license, because no company is holding anything on your behalf ([[custodial-vs-own-wallet]]).
